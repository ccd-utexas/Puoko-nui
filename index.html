White Dwarf Stars

Stars exist as a balance between gravity and the outflow of energy created from nuclear fusion within their core. When a star runs out of nuclear fuel it will evolve into a small and extremely dense stellar remnant: a white dwarf, neutron star, or black hole. The vast majority of stars (over 97%) are predicted to end their lives as a white dwarf.

The forces sustaining a white dwarf are dominated by quantum and relativistic effects. They contain a core about the size of the Earth which contains a classical gas of nuclides (mainly carbon and oxygen) and a quantum gas of electrons travelling near the speed of light. The core is covered with an atmosphere (of similar thickness to our own) containing mainly hydrogen and/or helium.

Most white dwarfs are relatively hot which makes them appear blue compared to other stars, but their small size makes them intrinsically faint. They slowly cool as they radiate away their internal energy, causing their radiation to shift to longer wavelengths (become redder) over time. The observed distribution of white dwarf temperatures provides an important constraint on the age of our galaxy.

As they cool, there are certain temperature regimes where the outflowing energy is able to interact with the hydrogen or helium in the atmosphere to drive complicated pulsations. The strong gravity of white dwarfs mean that these pulsations are largely non-radial. These pulsations manifest as periodic temperature variations at the surface, which cause the apparant brightness of the star to change on timescales of a few minutes. These brightness variations are made up of many sinusoidal frequencies which are related to the internal structure of the star.

We are able to identify the frequencies that a star pulsates at by measuring its intensity over time and using the Fourier transform to find the equivalent sinusoids that it contains. These frequencies can then be used to constrain numerical models of white dwarf interiors to indirectly measure important physical charactersics of the star. Some stars show non-sinusoidal pulsations, which is related to convection within the stellar atmosphere.

The short timescale of the pulsations means that we must continuosly measure intensities with integrations of tens of seconds. Most white dwarf research is done with ~1m class telescopes, and so it is important to have an efficient photometer that makes the most of the limited number of photons available.

Instrumentation

We have developed a flexible photometer system for measuring the intensity of variable stars. The photometer integrates an off-the-shelf CCD camera and GPS receiver with custom timing electronics and software for instrument control and data analysis.

Two variants of the instrument exist with different cameras. The original VUW system uses a Princeton Instruments MicroMax CCD, and is used with the 1m telescope at Mt John University Observatory. A second instrument is operated by the White Dwarf group at the University of Texas in Austin. This instrument uses a Princeton Instruments ProEM CCD, and has been used with the 82" and 36" telescopes at McDonald Observatory and the 24" telescope at Meyer Observatory.

The CCDs in both instruments are back-illuminated 1024×1024px frame-transfer CCDs. The back-illumination provides exceptional quantum efficiency in the blue wavelengths emitted by hot white dwarfs, and the frame-transfer operation allows the cameras to be operated without a mechanical shutter, further increasing the photon collecting ability of the system.

Our timing hardware is built around an Atmel AVR microprocessor (similar to those used in the Arduino platform) which communicates with an external GPS receiver. Precise exposure timing is available with a 1 millisecond resolution for exposures less than 65 seconds, or 1 second resolution for longer intervals. More information (including hardware specifications and source code) is available in the Karaka repository.

Instrument control software has been developed to run under Linux and Windows. More information (including source code) is available in the Puoko-nui repository.

tsreduce analyses data as it is acquired to provide real-time analysis of the target star. It performs differential aperture photometry on the target star plus several comparison stars to remove the effect of cloud and transparency variations. The online reduction display includes raw intensities, an estimate of the current atmospheric seeing, and time-series and Fourier Transform of the target star. More information is available in the tsreduce repository

We have also developed a microprocessor unit used to drive LEDs in a light-box for bench-testing the instrument and online reduction. More information is available in the Star Simulator repository.
